Manuscript: Normative Module Section
3. The Normative Module
3.1 Motivation
Machine-learning evaluations in clinical and high-stakes settings routinely treat fairness as a technical problem to be optimised. We argue this framing is a category error. Fairness criteria encode moral priorities: who is harmed by false negatives, who by false positives, and which harms the deploying institution is prepared to accept. These questions have answers that depend on the social context of deployment, the power relations between affected groups, and the values of the communities whose lives the model touches. No metric can resolve them.
The stratum-eval normative module operationalises this insight through two components: the Normative Specification Document (NSD) and the Chouldechova impossibility visualiser. Together they force researchers to make their normative choices legible, auditable, and time-bounded.
3.2 The SVSP Protocol
The Stakeholder Verification and Sign-off Protocol (SVSP) governs how an NSD is created. It has four stages.
Stage 1 — Stakeholder mapping. Before any modelling begins, the evaluation team enumerates every group that could be materially affected by the model's outputs. This includes groups that will be directly scored, groups affected by downstream decisions (e.g. family members of patients), and groups whose under-representation in training data creates differential uncertainty.
Stage 2 — Consultation. Each identified group is consulted through a method appropriate to their capacity and context: structured interviews, focus groups, or validated survey instruments. The NSD records the consultation date, method, and affiliation for each stakeholder. Groups that were not consulted must also be recorded, with an explicit account of why and what will be done to address the gap.
Stage 3 — Normative deliberation. The evaluation team and stakeholder representatives deliberate on the choice of fairness criterion. The impossibility visualiser (§3.3) is presented at this stage to ensure all parties understand what the choice costs groups not favoured by it. The deliberation is documented in the fairness_rationale field. A rationale that does not name the trade-off is rejected by the validator as a hard error.
Stage 4 — Sign-off and horizon-setting. The NSD is finalised with a validity_horizon — a future date by which the normative context must be re-examined — and a sunset_condition specifying the operational trigger for mandatory withdrawal. The evaluation will not run past the validity horizon without a renewed NSD.
3.3 NSD Fields and Their Rationale
Every field in the NSD is required. Missing fields raise StratumNSDError and halt the evaluation. The design choice is deliberate: we treat a missing normative declaration the same way a compiler treats a missing type annotation when strict mode is enabled — as evidence that the author has not yet done the work, not as an invitation to supply a default.
The fields and their rationale are as follows.
use_case documents what the model decides or informs in plain language. It anchors the rest of the document to a concrete social action rather than an abstract prediction task.
fairness_criterion encodes the moral priority chosen from the three canonical options: equalized odds (equal FNR and FPR across groups), calibration (predicted probabilities reflect true conditional risk regardless of group), or demographic parity (equal selection rates). The choice is a Literal type enforced at parse time.
fairness_rationale is the most important field. It must acknowledge, in text, what the chosen criterion costs groups not favoured by it. The validator checks for the presence of trade-off language and rejects documents that omit it. This requirement exists because in our experience the most common failure mode in fairness documentation is not dishonesty but incompleteness: researchers choose a criterion without having thought through its costs.
acceptable_fnr and acceptable_fpr record the thresholds agreed upon through stakeholder consultation. They are not model hyperparameters — they are social contracts. A model that exceeds these thresholds in any group triggers the monitoring escalation protocol.
stakeholders_represented and stakeholders_not_represented together constitute a complete account of whose values were and were not incorporated into the normative decision. Both fields are required. An empty stakeholders_not_represented list is an explicit claim that no relevant group was excluded — a claim that can be audited.
monitoring_frequency specifies the cadence of post-deployment performance review. It is a string rather than an enum to accommodate the diversity of deployment contexts: some deployments are better monitored by prediction volume than by calendar time.
validity_horizon is the most operationally novel field in the NSD. It encodes the recognition that normative contexts change: communities' priorities shift, disease prevalence changes, institutional power relations evolve. An NSD valid today may be invalid in two years. The validity horizon makes this impermanence explicit and forces a scheduled re-examination.
sunset_condition specifies the condition under which the model must be withdrawn regardless of calendar time. It complements the validity horizon by providing a trigger-based rather than schedule-based withdrawal mechanism.
3.4 Who Decides the Acceptable FNR?
This question is the central normative question in clinical risk model deployment, and the NSD's answer is: not the modelling team alone.
A false negative in a sepsis risk model means a patient who has sepsis is not flagged for escalated care. The cost of that error is borne by the patient. The acceptable rate of such errors is therefore a question about how much harm to patients a clinical institution is prepared to accept — and patients, or their representatives, must be part of that determination. The NSD's stakeholders_represented field operationalises this requirement.
The modelling team's role is to translate the socially-negotiated acceptable FNR into a technical threshold and to verify that the model achieves it across all evaluated groups. When it does not — when, for example, the model achieves FNR = 0.12 for White patients but FNR = 0.21 for Black patients under the same operating threshold — the appropriate response is not to average across groups and declare success. It is to return to the stakeholder consultation process, document the disparity, and either adjust the threshold (accepting higher FPR in one group) or return the model for retraining.
3.5 Fairness Impossibility Subsection
The Impossibility Theorem and What the NSD Forces You to Document
In a 2017 paper that should be required reading for anyone deploying a risk score in a clinical setting, Alexandra Chouldechova proved the following: if a binary classifier is calibrated — meaning its predicted probabilities reflect true conditional risk — and if the base rate of the outcome differs across groups, then the classifier cannot simultaneously satisfy equalized odds. More precisely, it cannot achieve equal false-positive and false-negative rates across groups at the same time as achieving calibration.
The proof follows directly from the confusion matrix identities. For a group with base rate p and predicted positive rate p̂, the false-positive rate is determined by:
FPR = (p̂ − p · (1 − FNR)) / (1 − p)
If two groups have different base rates p₁ ≠ p₂, then holding FNR constant across groups while maintaining calibration forces their FPRs to differ. The mathematics is unambiguous. The implication for practice is that every deployed risk model is already making a normative choice — it simply may not be making it consciously or transparently.
This is not a problem that better algorithms will solve. It is a structural property of prediction under unequal prevalence. A model that achieves perfect calibration across groups will necessarily have unequal error rates. A model that achieves equal error rates will necessarily be miscalibrated in at least one group. There is no third option.
The stratum-eval impossibility visualiser makes this concrete by plotting the (FNR, FPR) frontier for each demographic group in the cohort. When base rates differ, the frontiers diverge: the operating point that achieves acceptable FNR for one group implies a different FPR for another. The jointly acceptable region — where all groups simultaneously fall within the agreed thresholds — may be small, or it may not exist at all. The visualiser shows the researcher whether their agreed thresholds are feasible before they attempt to fit a model.
What the NSD forces the researcher to document is the downstream consequence of this impossibility. By requiring a fairness_criterion field that accepts exactly one of equalized_odds, calibration, or demographic_parity, and by requiring a fairness_rationale that names the trade-off explicitly, the NSD ensures that the following questions have been answered and recorded:
If calibration was chosen: which groups bear higher false-positive or false-negative rates as a result? Have those groups been consulted about whether they find this acceptable? What are the downstream harms of elevated false-negative rates in those groups (missed diagnoses, delayed treatment, under-triage)?
If equalized odds was chosen: in which groups will the model be systematically miscalibrated? Will clinicians in those settings receive predicted probabilities that do not reflect true conditional risk? What decisions will be made on the basis of those miscalibrated scores?
If demographic parity was chosen: does equal selection rate across groups reflect equal need? In a clinical context where outcome prevalence differs, demographic parity may systematically under-resource groups with higher true need.
None of these questions have universal answers. The NSD does not attempt to answer them — it demands that the evaluation team answer them, in writing, in consultation with affected communities, before the evaluation runs. The impossibility theorem tells us that trade-offs are unavoidable. The NSD tells us that unavoidable trade-offs must be owned.
